## 1. Results Aggregation

### Script

`scripts/aggregate_results.py` written and added to the project repository. Reads
all `final_metrics.json` files from `results/training/`, assembles the complete
24-model table, computes summary statistics, flags cross-domain collapse patterns,
and writes four output files to `results/aggregated/`:

- `summary_table.csv` — flat 24-model table, machine-readable
- `within_site.csv` — within-site subset for paper Table 1
- `cross_domain.csv` — cross-domain subset for paper Table 2
- `full_results.json` — complete structured dump including per-class F1

old and new metric key names from evaluate.py versions.

the field is not currently written to `final_metrics.json` by evaluate.py. Does
not affect paper results; low-priority fix for a future session.

## 3. Within-Site Analysis

### Architecture Ranking

EfficientNetV2-S is the strongest architecture across all four subsets (mean macro
F1 = 0.6240), followed by ResNet50 (0.6152), then ViT-B/16 (0.5739). The ranking
is consistent — no subset reversal. This is the primary within-site conclusion.

### Site × Origin Pattern

Performance degrades consistently: JA natural ≈ JA lab > BE natural > BE lab.
BE lab is the hardest subset for all three architectures (F1 0.53–0.56), likely
reflecting BE's multi-supplier imaging producing a less learnable feature space
for lab diamonds specifically.

### ViT Exception

ViT's JA natural result (F1=0.5595) is its worst across all four subsets — the
only case where an architecture underperforms its own within-site mean. ViT
recovered on JA lab (0.6181), which is a larger split. Consistent with ViT
requiring more data than the JA natural split provides. One sentence in paper.

### mid_range Confirmed Hardest Class

Pre-registered prediction confirmed. mid_range F1 across all 12 within-site models:
min=0.3423, max=0.5279, mean=0.4415. mid_range ranks last or tied-last in every
single model without exception. Clean result.

### Gap to RF Ceiling

Visual models peak at F1=0.6724 vs RF ceiling 0.8567 (JA natural) — a ~19pp gap.
Correct framing: RF has direct access to carat, cut, color, clarity. Visual models
must infer those from images. The gap is expected and interpretable, not a failure.

## 4. Cross-Domain Analysis

### JA→BE Failure is Universal

All six JA→BE runs failed across all three architectures:

| Architecture | JA natural → BE | JA lab → BE |
|---|---|---|
| ResNet50 | 0.1538 | 0.1284 |
| EfficientNetV2-S | 0.0708 | 0.1371 |
| ViT-B/16 | 0.0872 | 0.1286 |

Mean JA→BE macro F1: **0.1177**. Against within-site baseline of 0.55–0.67, this
is near-total collapse. The result is architectural-agnostic — three different
model families all fail. This is the primary cross-domain finding.

**Nature of failure:** Budget class nearly vanishes (F1=0.0005–0.033) but the
collapse is distributed across classes rather than single-class mode collapse.
The attempt1_bad artifact (LR mismatch, 93% premium predictions) was a methodology
artifact — the real JA→BE results reflect genuine inability to separate the BE
distribution, not a degenerate prediction strategy.

### BE→JA Shows Partial Transfer

| Architecture | BE natural → JA | BE lab → JA |
|---|---|---|
| ResNet50 | 0.3097 | 0.2048 |
| EfficientNetV2-S | 0.3054 | 0.1411 |
| ViT-B/16 | 0.2162 | 0.2424 |

Mean BE→JA macro F1: **0.2366** — approximately 2× JA→BE. The asymmetry hypothesis
is confirmed: BE's multi-supplier imaging produces more generalizable visual features
than JA's controlled 40× photography regime.

### Natural > Lab Pattern in BE→JA

BE natural → JA transfers at F1=0.31/0.31 (ResNet50/EfficientNetV2). BE lab → JA
drops to 0.14/0.24. Lab diamonds have more uniform visual characteristics and less
surface variation — BE lab training produces features that are even more
supplier-specific than BE natural.

### investment_grade Transfers Best

investment_grade F1 is the highest cross-domain class in almost all BE→JA runs
(0.37–0.58). Large, visually distinctive stones likely image consistently regardless
of source. Budget fails most severely in JA→BE (F1 near zero) — the budget tier
boundary is the most site-specific, reflecting different price ranges and imaging
standards between retailers.

## 5. Domain Adaptation Experiment

### Design

Checkpoint: `results/training/efficientnetv2__ja_natural__cross/best_model.pth`
(baseline JA→BE F1 = 0.0708 — worst JA→BE result, maximum contrast for adaptation).

Fine-tuning pool: `data/splits/be_natural_test.csv` (16,043 images, never seen
during original training). Three sample sizes: N=500, 1000, 2000. 80/20 train/val
split within fine-tuning pool; remaining images form held-out eval set.

Configuration: LR=1e-5, frozen backbone except last two blocks + classifier head,
float32, patience=3, max_epochs=10 (extended to 20 for N=2000).

**Leakage verification:** All three N conditions confirmed clean by Claude Code.
ft_train ∩ ft_val = 0, ft_train ∩ eval = 0, ft_val ∩ eval = 0. ft_train + ft_val
+ eval = 16,043 = pool total (exact partition) for all N. Results are valid.

### Results

| Run | Fine-tune N | Epochs | Eval Macro F1 | Δ vs Baseline |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Baseline (zero-shot) | 0 | — | 0.0708 | — |
| N=500 | 400 train / 100 val | 10 | ~0.2615 | +0.1907 (3.7×) |
| N=1000 | 800 / 200 | 10 | ~0.3200 | +0.2492 |
| N=2000 | 1600 / 400 | 10 | 0.3723 | +0.3015 |
| N=2000 ext | 1600 / 400 | 20 | 0.4004 | +0.3296 |

Gains are monotonic with N. budget and investment_grade recover fastest (were
near-zero at baseline). premium lags — consistent with the JA model's prior bias
toward premium predictions.

### Convergence Status

N=2000 ext (20 epochs): best at epoch 19, expired patience at epoch 20. Still not
converged. Estimated asymptote ~0.42–0.43 based on diminishing returns
(+0.0281 gain per additional 10 epochs). Further epoch extension not warranted
given marginal gain; disclosed as known limitation.

> With as few as 500 target-domain samples, fine-tuning recovers performance to

## 6. Regression Experiment — Setup

### Motivation

Replace the 4-class classification head with a single output predicting log(price).
Swap cross-entropy for Huber loss. This sidesteps the tier boundary approximation
entirely and answers a cleaner question: can visual features predict continuous
diamond value?

### Verification (Terminal 2 pre-flight)

- `data/splits/ja_natural_train.csv` — 75,380 rows, `price_usd` column confirmed present
- `data/splits/be_natural_train.csv` — 74,865 rows, `price_usd` column confirmed present
- Head swap: `nn.Linear(1280, 4)` → `nn.Linear(1280, 1)`, `.squeeze(1)` at forward output

Price statistics:

| Stat | JA natural | BE natural |
|---|---|---|
| min | $180 | $260 |
| max | $497,090 | $293,550 |
| mean | $5,638 | $4,449 |
| median | $1,720 | $2,180 |
| std | $12,527 | $8,896 |
| log(price) mean | 7.69 | 7.74 |
| log(price) std | 1.25 | 1.05 |

Both distributions are heavily right-skewed (median ≪ mean). log(price) target
is well-conditioned (std ~1.05–1.25). Huber loss appropriate.

### Implementation Note

Shape mismatch caught during dry-run: model head outputs `[batch, 1]`, Huber loss
expects `[batch]`. `.squeeze(1)` was already present at both call sites in
`train_regression.py` (lines 190 and 231). Dry-run (1 epoch, batch=32) confirmed
forward path end-to-end clean. No actual shape error occurred during the real run.
